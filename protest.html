EDUCATION / POLITICS & SOCIETY · FOOTNOTE 001

Jantar Mantar, Uncensored

This was supposed to be about an examination. Somewhere along the way, it became a much larger question: what happens when students stop believing the system is listening?

It began with education.

At the centre of the demonstrations at Jantar Mantar is a demand that sounds deceptively simple: accountability.

Students and protesters have raised allegations of irregularities surrounding NEET-UG and wider failures in India's examination system.For students who spend years preparing for competetive examinations, an exam is not merely a test. It can decide access to a university, a profession, and, sometimes, an entirely different economic future.

That is why a paper leak or an allegedly compromised examination is not experienced simply as an administrative mistake. To a student, it can feel like the rules changed after they had already spent years following them.

When the rules fail, who carries the cost?

The demand for accountability

One name consequently moved to the centre of the demonstrations: Union Education Minister Dharmendra Pradhan.

Protesters demanded his resignation over alleged examination irregularities. The demand transformed the movement from a conversation about individual examinations into a larger argument about political responsibility.

Who is responsible when an institution fails? The agency conducting an examination? Officials overseeing it? The ministry? Or does responsibility become so distributed that eventually nobody is accountable at all?

WHY THIS MATTERS

Competitive examinations operate partly on trust. Students accept enormous competition because they are promised that everyone will ultimately be judged under the same rules. When that trust weakens, the damage extends beyond one examination cycle.

Then Sonam Wangchuk sat down.

The movement gained another dimension when education reformer and activist Sonam Wangchuk joined protesters at Jantar Mantar.

On June 28, Wangchuk began an indefinite hunger strike in solidarity with the movement and its demand for accountability over alleged examination irregularities.

His presence changed the symbolism of the protest. Wangchuk has spent decades associated with education reform. Now an educator was sitting beside students who believed the education system itself had failed them.

July 18

After roughly three weeks of fasting, the story changed again.

Delhi Police removed Wangchuk from Jantar Mantar and shifted him to Safdarjung Hospital. Authorities cited concerns about his health and medical advice.

Protesters and his supporters described the removal as forced. What followed was therefore not only a debate about Wangchuk's physical condition, but about something much harder to answer: where does the state's responsibility to protect a protester's health end, and its power to interrupt a protest begin?

TWO VERSIONS OF THE SAME MOMENT

Police framed the hospitalisation around medical necessity. Protesters objected to the manner in which Wangchuk was removed. Both claims matter. Reporting a protest means distinguishing between what authorities say happened and what protesters say happened instead of quietly turning either account into fact.

THE QUESTION

When does protecting a protester become controlling a protest?

The story became bigger than NEET.

By then, the demonstrations were no longer confined to a single examination controversy.

They had become a vehicle for a broader frustration among young Indians: repeated examination controversies, intense competition, uncertain employment and a feeling that the people most affected by institutional failures are often the last people invited into the rooms where those failures are discussed.

Protests spread beyond Delhi. Opposition politicians entered the story. Parliament became part of it. What began with students questioning an examination system increasingly became a national argument over how institutions respond when young people demand accountability.

But there is another footnote.

Political attention can amplify a student movement. It can also overshadow it.

Once ministers, opposition leaders and national parties enter the frame, the easiest story becomes government versus opposition. The harder story is still sitting underneath it: the students who were asking whether the system governing their futures deserved their trust.

That distinction matters because education controversies do not end when television cameras leave Jantar Mantar. Students return to classrooms. Applicants return to entrance examinations. Another batch begins preparing.

LOOK AGAIN

If the political story becomes louder than the students, whose protest are we actually covering?

The Footnote

Jantar Mantar is the headline.

The footnote is everything that made people arrive there: years of preparation, faith in competitive examinations, allegations of irregularities, demands for accountability, and a generation increasingly unwilling to treat institutional failure as something it must quietly absorb.

Sonam Wangchuk being taken from a hunger strike to a hospital became one of the movement's most visible moments. Dharmendra Pradhan became one of its most visible political names.

But neither is the entire story.

The story is what happens to trust when students begin to believe that effort alone is no longer enough.

And perhaps that is the question worth following long after Jantar Mantar disappears from the front page:

What does an education system owe the students who are asked to trust it with their futures?
