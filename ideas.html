IDEAS · FOOTNOTE 003

When merit becomes a privilege

We like to believe success belongs to the most capable. But what if opportunity begins long before merit is measured?

The comfort of merit

Merit is an attractive idea because it makes success feel fair. Work hard, perform well, and opportunity should follow. It gives achievement a simple explanation: the person who earned the most must have deserved the most.

But merit is rarely measured in isolation. A score, an interview, a competition or an application records performance at one moment. It does not automatically record everything that made that performance possible.

The competition begins before the exam

Imagine two students sitting the same examination. They receive the same paper, the same amount of time and the same marking scheme. On paper, the competition is equal.

But one may have had years of strong schooling, private coaching, reliable internet, books, a quiet room and enough time to study. Another may have spent those same years navigating weaker resources, responsibilities at home, financial pressure or limited access to guidance.

The exam may begin at the same hour. Their preparation for it did not.

THE QUESTION

If everyone crosses the same finish line, but they began from different starting points, what exactly are we measuring?

Privilege does not erase effort

Recognising advantage does not mean achievement is fake. Someone with opportunities can still work extraordinarily hard. The problem begins when access to those opportunities disappears from the story entirely.

Privilege is often most powerful when it looks ordinary: knowing which opportunities exist, having someone who can explain an application, being able to take an unpaid experience, or simply having enough time to fail once and try again.

These advantages do not guarantee success. But they can change how difficult success is to reach.

Who gets to look talented?

Institutions often encounter people only after years of unequal opportunity have already shaped them. By then, advantage can appear as confidence, polish, experience or achievement.

That creates a difficult possibility: sometimes systems designed to identify talent may also identify who had the greatest chance to develop and display that talent.

The distinction matters because potential and proof of potential are not always the same thing.

Equality is not always fairness

Treating everyone identically sounds fair. Sometimes it is. But identical rules cannot always correct unequal conditions that existed before those rules were applied.

This is why debates about merit become complicated so quickly. A society must decide whether fairness means giving everyone exactly the same test, or also asking whether everyone had a meaningful chance to prepare for it.

There may never be a perfect measurement of merit. But pretending that context does not exist does not make the measurement neutral.

Ideas are not meant merely to exist. They create the depth from which the essence of a revolution can emerge.

The footnote

Perhaps the problem is not merit itself. Effort, skill and achievement matter.

The problem begins when we treat achievement as though it emerged without context — when the final result becomes visible while the opportunities that helped create it disappear.

A fair society does not have to pretend that everyone is identical. It has to become better at recognising the distance different people had to travel.

Merit may tell us who succeeded. The footnote asks what made that success possible.
