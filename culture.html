CULTURE · FOOTNOTE 002

Who decides what a generation is remembered for?

Every generation gets a label. The harder question is who gets to choose it.

A generation becomes a headline

Generations are complicated. Millions of people grow up with different families, opportunities, fears and ambitions, yet we repeatedly compress them into a handful of words.

Lazy. Entitled. Rebellious. Sensitive. Radical.

These labels are convenient because they turn something enormous and contradictory into something that appears easy to understand. But a label is never the whole generation. It is an interpretation of it.

Who gets to describe whom?

The people living through a cultural shift are rarely the only people explaining it. Newspapers interpret them. Politicians speak about them. Algorithms amplify particular versions of them. Older generations compare them with their own memories.

Eventually repetition can begin to look like truth. A description repeated often enough becomes a stereotype, and a stereotype can become the story remembered long after the moment itself has passed.

THE QUESTION

If someone else writes the story of your generation, how much of your generation survives?

What the label leaves out

Calling a generation lazy tells us almost nothing about the world in which that generation is trying to live. It does not explain changing education systems, uncertain job markets, technology, political frustration or the cost of opportunity.

Calling young people rebellious can hide another possibility: perhaps they are responding to institutions that no longer make sense to them.

The interesting question is therefore not whether a stereotype is completely true or completely false. It is what disappears when the stereotype becomes the entire explanation.

Culture is also memory

Culture is not only music, fashion, films or language. It is also the way a society remembers itself.

Years from now, today's arguments will become somebody else's history. The posts, headlines, photographs and political speeches that survive will help determine how this generation is understood.

But archives preserve evidence, not necessarily experience. What it felt like to live through a moment can disappear even when thousands of records of that moment remain.

The footnote

Maybe generations should not be remembered by the loudest adjective attached to them.

Maybe the better record lies in the contradictions: what people feared, what they questioned, what they inherited, what they rejected, and what they tried to change.

A generation is not a label. It is millions of unfinished stories.

And perhaps that is what every generation eventually discovers: the power in people is greater than the people in power.
